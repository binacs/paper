# Cross-Layer Energy Analysis of Multimodal Training on Grace Hopper Superchips

## TL;DR

This paper presents a cross-layer energy analysis of multimodal training on NVIDIA Grace Hopper superchips, showing that energy efficiency is governed by data movement and overlap rather than raw compute utilization, and that runtime-optimal configurations are not necessarily energy-optimal.

## Problem & Motivation

Multimodal deep learning models scale rapidly, causing memory and communication bottlenecks. Training performance becomes limited by data movement rather than computation. Frameworks like DeepSpeed mitigate these issues via CPU offloading, activation checkpointing, and communication optimizations, but these techniques introduce additional system activity that may affect energy efficiency. There is a need to understand the energy-performance trade-offs in such heterogeneous architectures.

## Key Ideas

- Cross-layer analysis of energy and performance trade-offs in multimodal training on GH200 systems.
- Characterization of interactions between application, runtime, and hardware layers.
- Leveraging high-bandwidth CPU-GPU interconnects (NVLink-C2C) and unified memory.
- Study of offloading strategies, activation checkpointing, and sequence parallelism.
- Identification that energy efficiency is primarily governed by data movement and overlap, not raw compute utilization.
- Distillation of actionable guidelines for balancing offloading, parallelism, and scheduling.

## System Design

The system under study is the NVIDIA Grace Hopper (GH200) superchip, which integrates a Grace CPU and Hopper GPU with high-bandwidth NVLink-C2C interconnect and unified memory. The training pipeline uses DeepSpeed for CPU offloading, activation checkpointing, and communication optimizations. The analysis spans application (model architecture, data loading), runtime (DeepSpeed, CUDA), and hardware (GPU, CPU, memory, interconnect) layers.

## Evaluation

The paper evaluates multimodal training workloads (likely text, image, video) on GH200 systems. It compares various configurations of offloading, activation checkpointing, and sequence parallelism. Headline results show that energy efficiency is governed by data movement and overlap, and that runtime-optimal configurations are not energy-optimal. The paper provides practical guidelines for achieving a balance among energy efficiency, runtime performance, and throughput.

## Takeaways

- Energy efficiency in multimodal training on GH200 is primarily determined by data movement and overlap, not compute utilization.
- Runtime-optimal configurations may be suboptimal for energy; practitioners must consider both metrics.
- High-bandwidth CPU-GPU interconnects enable effective offloading and sequence parallelism without severe energy penalties.
- Cross-layer analysis is essential to understand trade-offs in heterogeneous systems.

## Related Work

The paper positions itself against prior work on DeepSpeed optimizations (CPU offloading, activation checkpointing, communication optimizations) and studies on energy efficiency in deep learning training. It extends these by focusing on the GH200 architecture and providing cross-layer energy analysis.

## Open Questions

- How do the findings generalize to other heterogeneous architectures (e.g., AMD MI300, Intel Ponte Vecchio)?
- What is the impact of different multimodal model architectures (e.g., fusion strategies) on energy efficiency?
- Can the guidelines be automated into a runtime scheduler for energy-aware training?
- How does energy efficiency scale with larger model sizes and longer sequences beyond those tested?
