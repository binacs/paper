# Federated Optimization in Heterogeneous Networks

## TL;DR

FedProx generalizes FedAvg to handle systems and statistical heterogeneity in federated networks by adding a proximal term and allowing variable local work, achieving more robust convergence.

## Problem & Motivation

Federated learning faces two key challenges: systems heterogeneity (variability in device capabilities) and statistical heterogeneity (non-i.i.d. data across devices). Existing methods like FedAvg struggle with convergence under these conditions, often diverging or requiring careful tuning.

## Key Ideas

- Introduce a proximal term to the local objective to limit the impact of local updates.
- Allow each device to perform a variable amount of local work based on its systems constraints.
- Reformulate FedAvg as a special case of FedProx, providing a unified framework.

## System Design

FedProx modifies the local training step: each device minimizes the original loss plus a proximal term that penalizes deviation from the global model. The server aggregates updates as in FedAvg, but devices can perform different numbers of local iterations. The proximal term is controlled by a hyperparameter μ.

## Evaluation

Evaluated on realistic federated datasets (e.g., synthetic, MNIST, Shakespeare, FEMNIST) with varying degrees of heterogeneity. Baselines include FedAvg and other methods. In highly heterogeneous settings, FedProx improves absolute test accuracy by 22% on average over FedAvg, with more stable convergence.

## Takeaways

- A simple proximal modification can significantly improve robustness to heterogeneity.
- Variable local work is crucial for systems heterogeneity.
- FedProx provides theoretical convergence guarantees under both statistical and systems heterogeneity.

## Related Work

Extends FedAvg, the state-of-the-art federated learning method. Addresses limitations of FedAvg in heterogeneous settings, contrasting with prior work that assumes i.i.d. data or uniform device participation.

## Open Questions

Optimal tuning of the proximal term μ; extension to more complex aggregation rules; handling of stragglers and device failures; scalability to extremely large networks.
