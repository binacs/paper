# Distributed Quantum Circuit Optimisation: Evaluating Global and Local encodings

## TL;DR

This paper studies how circuit optimisation interacts with distributed quantum circuit execution, comparing global, local, and hybrid compilation strategies under telegate-based partitioning. It finds that no single strategy uniformly benefits all metrics, with global optimisation minimising computational cost, local optimisation reducing communication, and hybrid offering balanced improvements at the cost of high compilation overhead.

## Problem & Motivation

As distributed quantum architectures emerge, optimising quantum circuits for distributed execution involves trade-offs between computational cost (gate counts, depth), communication cost (non-local gates), and compilation overhead. Prior work often treats optimisation and partitioning separately; this paper investigates their interaction systematically.

## Key Ideas

- Compare three compilation strategies: global optimisation (optimise whole circuit then partition), local optimisation (partition first then optimise each part independently), and hybrid (optimise globally, partition, then optimise locally).
- Use telegate-based partitioning to split circuits into distributed parts.
- Evaluate on a large benchmark suite of quantum algorithms across gate counts, circuit depth, number of non-local gates, and compilation time.

## System Design

The system takes a quantum circuit as input, applies one of three compilation strategies, then partitions the circuit using telegate-based partitioning. The output is a distributed circuit. Metrics: gate counts, circuit depth, number of non-local gates (communication cost), and compilation overhead (classical preprocessing time).

## Evaluation

Evaluated on a large benchmark suite of quantum algorithms. Baselines are the three strategies compared against each other. Headline results: global optimisation minimises computational resources and compilation overhead; local optimisation reduces communication cost; hybrid reduces both computational and communication overhead but with significantly increased compilation time.

## Takeaways

- Circuit optimisation does not uniformly benefit distributed execution; the best strategy depends on the metric of interest.
- Global optimisation is best for minimising computational cost and compilation time.
- Local optimisation can reduce communication even without explicit communication-awareness.
- Hybrid approach offers balanced improvements but at high compilation cost.

## Related Work

The paper positions itself within the emerging field of distributed quantum computing, focusing on the interaction between circuit optimisation and partitioning. It does not name specific related systems but addresses a gap in understanding trade-offs.

## Open Questions

- How to adapt strategies for different network topologies or hardware constraints?
- Can compilation overhead of hybrid approach be reduced?
- How do these strategies scale with larger numbers of qubits and partitions?
