# On the Distortion of Partitioning Performance by Random Quantum Circuits

## TL;DR

This paper shows that using random quantum circuits to benchmark hypergraph partitioners for distributed quantum computing distorts performance evaluation, inflating cut costs and altering strategy rankings, whereas structured generated circuits better approximate real workloads.

## Problem & Motivation

Hypergraph partitioning is critical for distributed quantum computing compilers, but the limited size of quantum benchmark suites forces researchers to rely on random quantum circuits for evaluation. This raises the question of whether such benchmarking practices provide a faithful assessment of partitioner performance, as random circuits may not represent real algorithmic workloads.

## Key Ideas

- Compare hypergraph partitioning strategies across three circuit origins: real algorithmic circuits, structured generated circuits, and fully random circuits.
- Measure cut costs, scaling trends across QPU counts and circuit sizes, and relative ranking of partitioning strategies.
- Quantify distortion introduced by random circuits versus structured generated circuits.

## Evaluation

Evaluated a diverse set of state-of-the-art hypergraph partitioning strategies on circuits from three origins. Headline results: random circuits inflate cut costs, alter scaling trends, and change relative rankings; structured generated circuits exhibit substantially lower distortion and more closely approximate real workload behaviour.

## Takeaways

- Random circuits significantly distort partitioning evaluation in DQC.
- Structured generated circuits are a better proxy for real workloads.
- Benchmark selection directly influences methodological conclusions in DQC research.
- Using random circuits may provide misleading guidance for compiler design.

## Related Work

The paper positions itself against common benchmarking practices in DQC that rely on random circuits, highlighting the need for more representative workloads.

## Open Questions

What specific structured generation methods best approximate real workloads? How can the community develop larger benchmark suites of real algorithmic circuits?
