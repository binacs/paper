# Distributed Observer-based Fault Detection over Intelligent Networked Multi-Vehicle Systems

## TL;DR

This paper proposes a distributed fault detection and isolation (FDI) framework for mixed traffic networks of human-driven and autonomous vehicles, using consensus-based observers and probabilistic thresholds on residuals without requiring bounded noise assumptions.

## Problem & Motivation

In mixed traffic networks with human-driven vehicles (HDVs) and connected autonomous vehicles (CAVs), decentralized decision-making is crucial. However, CAVs have only partial sensor measurements, and the group of HDVs may not be locally observable in any CAV's direct neighborhood. Additionally, sensor faults or attacks can compromise safety, and existing deterministic threshold FDI methods assume bounded noise, which is unrealistic in real-world transportation systems.

## Key Ideas

- Distributed consensus-based observers enable CAVs to estimate the state of HDVs despite partial observability.
- Residual-based fault detection and isolation (FDI) is performed locally at each CAV without a central unit.
- Two FDI logics are proposed: one without history (instantaneous) and one with history (cumulative).
- Probabilistic thresholds on residuals are designed, relaxing the bounded noise assumption common in deterministic methods.

## System Design

The system consists of a mixed traffic network with HDVs and CAVs. Each CAV runs a distributed consensus-based observer to estimate the states of HDVs using local sensor measurements and communication with neighboring CAVs. The observer generates residuals (differences between measured and estimated outputs). Each CAV then applies a probabilistic threshold to the residual to decide if a fault/attack is present. Two logics are used: one based on the current residual value, and another that accumulates residual history for improved detection.

## Evaluation

The paper validates the approach through simulations of mixed traffic networks. It compares the proposed probabilistic threshold FDI with deterministic threshold methods, showing improved detection performance under realistic noise conditions. Headline numbers from the abstract are not provided, but the method is claimed to be more realistic for real-world systems.

## Takeaways

- Distributed observers can enable fault detection even when the target group is not locally observable.
- Probabilistic thresholds are more practical than deterministic ones when noise bounds are unknown.
- Incorporating residual history improves fault detection accuracy.
- The approach is fully decentralized, enhancing scalability and robustness.

## Related Work

The paper positions itself against existing centralized FDI methods and deterministic threshold techniques. It extends distributed observer-based approaches to fault detection in mixed traffic, addressing the limitation of local observability and bounded noise assumptions.

## Open Questions

- How does the method perform under different noise distributions and attack models?
- What is the communication overhead and convergence time of the distributed observers?
- Can the approach be extended to detect faults in actuators or communication links?
- How does the system scale with the number of vehicles and network topology?
